# Menopause Burnt Me First, Then Hurt Me in Other Ways
## My journey through ten years of unexpected symptoms, struggles, and finally — acceptance.

Yes, you read that correctly, menopause burnt me first, then it showed other signs.

I expected the menopause to arrive with hot flashes, night sweats, and mood swings, like for everyone else. However, mine announced itself in the middle of the night with an unbearable burning at the tip of my tongue. There was no warning, no prior indication.

### How it Began
It all started ten years ago when I was least expecting it. I was 38 then, and perimenopause was the last thing on my mind. Since then, it has tested me in ways I never imagined.

The tip of my tongue burned like it was on fire. I leapt out of bed, raided the fridge, rubbed ice cubes on it, drank chilled water, and stuck my tongue in front of a table fan like a dog trying to cool itself down. Nothing worked.

The next day, my doctor prescribed vitamin B12 for a ten-day course. To my relief, the burning disappeared, and I thought it was gone for good — until after six months, it came back with the same vengeance, again in the middle of the night. This time, however, B12 did not work.

### My Search for Answers
I went from doctor to doctor, including Ayurvedic practitioners, and even to a lady who claimed she could help me through Pranic Healing, which I don’t really know what that is, trying to figure out what was happening to me. And of course, Google was always there to confidently diagnose me with diseases I had never heard of.

Then started the frustrating cycles of tests and desperate experiments:

I changed toothpaste every week, suspecting I might have developed an allergy.
I had my wisdom tooth removed a couple of weeks back, so one doctor suggested anesthesia might have damaged a nerve.
I tried supplements, anxiety medication, and even hormone replacement therapy (HRT). Nothing worked.
The only temporary relief came from chewing gum. I basically turned into a cow ruminating to keep the fire away. But by evening, the burning returned worse than before.
Some days were worse than others. The burning used to be so severe that I could not speak. I wrote on a whiteboard whenever I wanted to say something to my husband and son. I stopped calling my parents. We started texting each other.

Every doctor told me the same thing: they had never seen a case like mine.

### The Mystery Continued
At 43, like it had come unannounced, it went away unannounced. It still comes back sometimes, just to say hello, lest I forget; otherwise, it is out of my mind now.

But my legs started burning from the knees down.

This came right after COVID, when my vitamin levels (B12 and D3) were very low. Again, the same rounds of check-ups and tests began. Again, with no real answer. Supplements, stretching exercises, and lifting weights have given me some relief, but my left ankle remains a little swollen and painful. Occasionally, both legs burn, though it’s less frequent. Compared to the tongue pain, this is manageable.

### The Weight Struggle
Just as I learned to live with the burning, another challenge appeared: weight gain.

I’ve always struggled with weight, but I could control it — until now. Keto once helped me lose 8 kilos in four months; today it does nothing. Intermittent fasting worked in the past; now it doesn’t move the scale. My body no longer responds the way it used to, no matter what I try.

### The Foggy Mind
Then came the “fogginess.” My memory is fine, but sometimes, mid-sentence, I blank out, and I cannot find the right words, the simplest ones. Though it lasts only for a fraction of a second, it’s unsettling.

### What I Did Not Experience
I am one of those who have never had PMS. I never had nausea during pregnancy. Even now, I do not know what hot flashes feel like. I have no sleep problems or night sweats. Instead, menopause found me in its own way: burning tongue, burning legs, stubborn weight, and foggy brain, though fleeting.

### Making Peace
After ten years, I have made peace with it. I say to myself: This is just another wave of perimenopause.

Now I focus on food. I try to eat balanced meals, three times a day. I am not punishing myself with all sorts of diets that I have tried before.

Exercise has become a routine. It helps to keep pain under control and my body strong.

Sleep has never been a problem for me, but I am now extra careful with the timings. I try to sleep exactly at the same time and have eight hours each night.

I am practicing giving up control and not taking life too seriously.

### What I learnt
Menopause burnt me first, then hurt me in other ways — but it also taught me resilience.

Menopause does not follow a script. We are all experiencing it in our own unique ways. It is indeed confusing and at times, overwhelming.

But this too shall pass. We will all come out of it becoming stronger.
