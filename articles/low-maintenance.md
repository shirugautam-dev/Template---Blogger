# Low-Maintenance Is a Lie We Tell Ourselves

Have you ever felt like you have not expressed enough? That you held it, worrying that the others would dislike the idea even if that’s what you wanted. How many times have you said “it’s ok” when it was not? “Anything is fine with me.” Really? You have no preference?

It has happened to me many times. I have laughed at jokes that I have not found funny and wanted to stare blankly at the speaker. I have said “It’s ok, I don’t mind” when I did mind that the plan was changed without asking if I liked it that way. I have kept my mouth shut even when I wanted to shout that it was a terrible idea. I have said “anything is fine” when I wanted to eat noodles, not “anything”. I have engaged in emotional conversations that never made it to my side of the table. And what for? To keep the peace. Not to come out looking like a difficult person. To be seen as easy-going. Ever laughing, ever happy. And what has it cost me? The very peace I wanted to keep. Because I brood over it for a long time.

It’s tough being easy — full-time pretending that it does not bother. We want that badge of honour — “Low-Maintenance”. Being “chill” may look nice to others, but for ourselves, it’s turmoil if we really are not. Convincing ourselves that we do not have needs and we can just go along with whatever is planned is the most devastating thing for our mental health. This constant emotional editing, strategic silence, eats us from the inside. Resentment builds up, and one day we retaliate in the way we never thought we could.

### Being Easy-going is a Performance, not a Personality Trait

Inherently, we try to adapt, be agreeable and be grateful for whatever is available to us because we do not want to be a burden. When we are constantly trying to manage our needs, “it’s fine” becomes so common that it becomes reflexive. We do not even spend a minute to think what it is that we want. We do not observe ourselves; we only observe others’ needs. It’s easier that way. Explaining why we need something differently takes effort, and we want to save on that energy. When we practice this kind of restraint for a long time, it becomes a performance. We should not mistake it for a personality trait.

### Low-Maintenance is Often Self-imposed Control

Low-maintenance comes with a high cost of self-control to suppress our needs constantly. It is exhausting to perform all the time. Tone, timing and delivery need to be thought again and again. Every conversation is emotionally buffered before it happens. And afterwards, we compose a whole conversation internally about why we mind and what we need.

We are bothered, but we do not want to look into it. We brush it off, saying it is not important. We do not want to inconvenience others, and that becomes the priority. What follows is not harmony but deep-rooted resentment.

Are we really a low effort? Not really. We are just good at hiding our effort.

We must understand our hunger for praise. Trying not to disturb the routines, not demanding personal space, and not challenging something that bothers us are not peace-keeping solutions. They are people-pleasing traits deeply rooted in learned self-protection — a pattern where we feel that shrinking keeps us safe. We compromise and then feel sorry for ourselves. We pat ourselves for being better than others as we sacrifice for their comfort.

Why are our preferences negotiable? Is it because of others, or is it self-imposed? Why is it so scary to ask that? It is scary because then we will see the fabrication that our thoughts create. It creates the desire to be liked, to be clapped, making us do things that we do not want to do.

### Flexibility Is Not the Same as Disappearing

There is a thin line between being flexible and invisible. Being flexible gives us the choice, whereas if being “low maintenance” makes us invisible, then it is self-sabotaging. Low-maintenance is not about having no needs; it is about focusing on realistic, simple, no-drama expectations where there is mutual support and flexibility. It is a way for others to offer us what we have offered them.

We need to be real and honest about our needs without being demanding. No one is here to serve us, but no one should discount us either. It means saying “I want Chinese tonight” instead of “Whatever you want”. It means admitting “Actually, that does not work for me” when plans do not suit us. It means recognising that having preferences does not make us difficult — it makes us human.

Let’s be “chill” but not silent.

Let’s stop trying to be easy. There should be no trying at all.
