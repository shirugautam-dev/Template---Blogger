# We All Are Performing on Social Media

Two decades back, we were trying to figure out how to play around with social media. We were new to Facebook. It let us connect with our friends and families that we had lost touch with. We were frantically looking for the people we once knew, and that was genuinely valuable. But at present, social media is taking an undeserved amount of time from our lives. We are no longer looking there for others; we are looking for ourselves. What was once a playful pastime has now become a tool to reshape our identities. Especially for the ones born within these two decades, it has become part of their identity development process. The benefit of this is controversial, but the fact that it is reshaping how we see ourselves and how we want to be seen cannot be denied.

### Performing Online
Last week I opened FB after a long time. I was surprised to see that it was flooded with advertisements. They were for the things I might have checked before ordering on Amazon, recipes I had googled and places I was thinking of holidaying. But more than that, I was zapped to see my aunties and cousins and friends in reels — versions of them I had never seen before. Some were great, some were okay, and some were a little awkward. But all the same. Trying to create a new version of themselves online. Reshaping their identities. The images were filtered, the captions were researched, and the moments were curated to show something that was not entirely them. I could imagine them waiting on the other side for the beeps of likes and comments. Their mood for the day decided by the whims of others.

This incomplete or even false identity that is being created online has started blurring the line between authenticity and projection. One may even start asking, “Am I the one who is online, or the one offline?” We are managing our identity rather than simply living it.

### Algorithm: The Powerhouse
I have been searching for strength training lately, and now all my online feeds are suggestions related to that. The algorithm shows everything based on my past searches. It is narrowing my interests and giving me curated lists. The more I engage, the more I start to feel that this is the core part of my identity. But I am not just my exercise routine, I am much more than that. The other parts of my life remain completely hidden.

Our identities are increasingly getting reshaped by the algorithms. We are getting feedback from them according to our behaviours, reinforcing those behaviours even further.

### Hungry for Validation and Approval
The moment we post something on social media, we wait for the likes, views, follows, and shares. We get addicted to these validations. Our self-esteem goes up and down several times a day. We begin adjusting our behaviour and opinions based on the online praises earned. This gradually starts shaping our identity. Personal values and confidence take a back seat.

We feel as if we need to curate a new self, a version that is approved online and crafted for strangers.

### The Desire to Conform
We want to be in groups; we want to belong. We want to stay relevant. We try our best to fit in. We surrender to the trend. We adapt to the habits or beliefs that are viral. All these can sometimes override the desire to be authentic. We let the platforms make us rather than we make the platforms.

We let our identities be in a public archive that needs to be managed. We have to look professional on LinkedIn, happy on FB, stylish on Instagram and perform on TikTok. We are checking on our friends and colleagues, and they are checking on us.

We are reinventing ourselves each day, trying to impress the outside world, forgetting that there is so much within us.

### More Authentic for Some
Despite all of this, for some, online versions of themselves can be more authentic than the offline ones if they are misunderstood or unheard otherwise. It becomes a platform to voice their opinion unfiltered, validate their feelings and find a space where they can grow. Niche online communities, support groups, and activist pages are there to offer deep connection and belonging when these are absent offline.

In that way, sometimes, online self becomes more honest and freer than the offline one, allowing people to explore their true selves.

### Finding Balance
The influence of social media cannot be measured in black and white. It is neither entirely negative nor entirely positive. It compares, it pressurises, but at the same time, it also gives space for creativity, to connect and to explore oneself.

Balance comes from recognising when it supports our growth and when it pulls us away from ourselves.

### Let’s Perform with Intention
Social media is reshaping how we think, how we work and how we connect. It has given us the world that was otherwise beyond our reach. In the process, however, it has also made us more vulnerable and less authentic.

We need to be aware of how the algorithm is influencing us, how the external validations are affecting our psyche and how our increased visibility is exposed to constant judgment.

We can curate our digital selves with intention, resist following the trends blindly and stay true to who we are.
