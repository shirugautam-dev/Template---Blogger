# Imposter Syndrome: Do You Feel It Too, or Am I the Only One?

I still remember the year 2002 — the year I wrote my final exam of BIT, results were four months away, and I found myself offered a teaching job at Southwestern Sydney Institute of TAFE — a job I didn’t feel ready for at all. The offer was good. It paid more than double the wage I was earning otherwise at Burger King. I took it right away. But the doubt crept in.

My friends, who were still looking for a break, celebrated. They congratulated me and said that I deserved it. I smiled, but inside I was panicking. My stomach twitched as if I had swallowed something rotten.

Half of my students were older than me — most of them taking the vocational courses to switch careers mid-life. All the other staff members were middle-aged. On the first day, the principal called me into her office and said these exact words. “You have to control the class; otherwise, they will control you”. That afternoon, I went out and bought glasses, just to look a few years older — maybe even capable.

I got along well with others; students seemed to like my classes. However, I never felt I deserved it. I felt it was just luck that got me there. I was hesitant. Deep down, I thought it was not my achievement; it was a fluke. The success did not feel comfortable, as if I had fooled myself and the others. It was like someone would catch me someday, that I was not as capable as they had thought I was. And the feeling remained till the end, even though I fared quite well in the job.

Many years later, I learned that it is called Imposter Syndrome, a term I did not know before. I realised I had the syndrome many times before that job and many times after as well. And also, it’s far more common than I knew.

### What is Imposter Syndrome and Why We Feel This Way?

Imposter Syndrome is the persistent feeling of not belonging or not deserving what we have achieved. There is always self-doubt. It is a tendency to have negative self-perception. We like to attribute to luck, a fluke or some other external factor for the accomplishments rather than to our abilities.

According to psychologists, there are many reasons why we feel this way. Our upbringing is often one of them. Extreme pressure to perform well in school, being judged solely by grades, and comparison among siblings are a few examples that may spiral into this continuous negative self-talk.

How we are attached to our loved ones or our possessions can also trigger this syndrome. If we are anxious about them, if we need constant reassurance, we may develop the feeling that we are not good enough.

The societal pressure to prove that we deserve everything that we achieve, the need to be endlessly productive at work and the never-ending comparison with others, our cultures and religious beliefs also feed this feeling of lack.

Looking back now, I know I was not incapable; I was just not able to see myself as capable.

### What Changed and What Helped?

One day, a few years back, a friend who is highly successful in his chosen career mentioned how he felt that it was not him. As if he were acting all the time. Suddenly, I knew I was not the only one.

I started noticing that mistakes do not always ruin things. They may delay the progress, change the course of action, but it is not the end of the world. Better still, they teach valuable lessons we otherwise would not have learned.

The feeling has not totally gone yet, but now I am more aware. I know it’s just a feeling of doubt. I need not stop or leave the battleground. There is no need to silence the voice — just to keep moving despite it.

### Now I Move Before I Feel Ready

Though the doubt still creeps in at times, I remind myself that not feeling ready does not mean I cannot take on a challenge. I can take on a challenge, and readiness can come after. From the girl who bought glasses just to appear older, I have come a long way and thinking that makes me smile. The discomfort I once felt did not mean I was not good enough; it meant I was still learning. Today, I do not wait for confidence to push me forward. I move, and the confidence follows slowly.
