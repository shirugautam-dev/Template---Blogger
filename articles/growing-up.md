# Growing Up with My 20-Year-Old

## Lessons in Love, Letting Go, and Late-Night Biryani

### Growing Up Together: Parenting Across Generations
The other night, I heard my son’s guitar humming through the walls at 2 a.m. He was laughing, probably on Discord with his friends. I smiled, realising we live together, yet we live worlds apart.

Parenting has changed across generations. Just as my mother grew with me, I am now growing with my son. When my mom became a parent, she was barely an adult herself, still learning how to take care of a tiny life in her arms while figuring out her own place in the world.

Now, decades later, I find myself in a similar place, learning to adapt and grow with my twenty-year-old son in a world that is nothing like the one I grew up in.

### Different Time, Different Rhythm
I have not lived with my son for about six months now, though we share the same roof. He is nocturnal, staying up late into the night, while I live during the afternoons. It feels as if we are renting the same space, but one person at a time. On my lucky mornings, I catch him rushing past the main door without a word. “That’s okay,” I tell myself. “At least he’s attending classes today.”

In rare moments, when we do cross paths, our conversations are brief, sometimes about classes, sometimes about food.
“Momma, you don’t get it — it’s not like your time,” he tells me when I remind him to slow down and be present. He’s right. I don’t. But I’m trying.

I am learning to temper my expectations and let him live his own rhythm. And while adjusting to his rhythm, I am also trying to adjust to his relationships.

### His Relationship, My Insecurity
A few months ago, he got his first girlfriend — a dream he’d had since first grade. On Valentine’s Day, he got up at 10 a.m. — much earlier than usual, dressed up, and dashed out, leaving a trail of perfume behind. I called him, curious. Over the phone, he proudly announced he was off on his very first date.

I couldn’t help but compare myself with my mother. Parents then might have panicked, offered warnings, or even tried to interfere.

“Oh,” I laughed over the phone, a reflex of surprise and amusement.

After he hung up, doubts crept in. Should I worry? Will he focus more on puppy love than studies? Should I discuss protection, or would that be too soon? Over the years, I had taken on that responsibility because his father, being shyer about such topics, never did.

That evening, he came home beaming. He told me everything — or at least I would like to think he did. I listened, smiled, and said simply, “Enjoy.”

Four months later, they broke up. I was a little disappointed — why? How? Is it that simple? — but I accepted it.

Today, I watch my son explore his own relationships, hoping he can experience them freely, without fear.

### Midnight Kitchens and the Smell of Biryani
Just a few nights ago, I woke up to the aroma of something. I found four kids sitting on the floor of our 8x12 kitchen, waiting for a pressure cooker to whistle, and enough laughter to wake up neighbours.

“What’s cooking?” I asked.
“Biryani,” he smiled. “Want some?”

It was 12:15 a.m. I had gone to bed at 10:30 p.m., leaving behind just one kid, glued to his phone. His friends come and go at odd hours; loud music fills the kitchen as he experiments late at night. My husband and I sleep through it, the fan drowning the noise. Still, I find comfort in knowing he is eating home-cooked meals and spending time in safe spaces — unlike the uncertainty of the outside world.

### An Adult, But Not Quite
He drives through city traffic, opens bank accounts, and books airline tickets without anyone’s help. Yet he panics over a t-shirt that costs 600 bucks. “It’s too expensive”, he declares. Half an hour later, I get a message: Same 600 spent at Starbucks. Apparently, 600 is inexpensive when it is brewed and served in a paper cup.

He talks about responsibilities, finding a job, and moving out — but still expects others to take care of him. He cannot yet part with his Batman figurines. Part of him clings to the safety of being a child.

### Knows Everything, Knows Nothing
He talks crypto, designs semiconductors, understands algorithms, and wants to invest in the Nifty Fifty. He knows how to answer big questions about life.

“It’s not real,” he says. “Our thoughts created it.”

But he panics when his phone dies. Ask him what he truly wants, and he’s clueless. He is old enough to explain life, too young to live it that way.

### Learning Beside Him
I am learning patience, acceptance, and a new rhythm of parenting. Growing alongside my son, I see a world of possibilities for him that I never experienced at his age. I do not want to instil fear or control; I want to walk beside him — close enough to support, far enough to let him find his own way.

I now understand that parenting doesn’t end — it transforms, from teaching to listening, guiding to watching. Just as my mother grew with me once, I am now growing with my son, discovering what it truly means to be a parent in an ever-changing world.
