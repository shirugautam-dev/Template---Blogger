# Imagining My Older Self and Accepting It Gracefully

It was Diwali. Since I am not religious, I like keeping it simple. Earlier, I would cook delicacies. But this year, I decided not to do so, as I was the only one who ended up eating them, gaining a few pounds later. That morning, while still in bed, I thought about what to cook — just aloo bhaji, poori and rice kheer.

When my husband and I were ready to eat, my son was still sleeping. Kheer and bhaji were done by then. I rolled eight poories and he fried them. We sat, ate and took turns in praising our food 😊. Then we took a bowl of kheer each and moved to the sofa.

“This is it, D, just us. This is old age,” I said. D is the nickname I gave him two decades ago.

“Yes, just us.” He replied with a smile.

I imagined our older selves — just the two of us, deciding what to eat, where to go, how to spend our time. We are both far away from our hometowns, and the kind of dependency we now feel is deeper than before. This is a dependency of companionship.

We dream of youth and the adventures that come with it, rarely thinking of old age. We chase after money as if it will take care of everything. But as we near our fifties, reality sets in. Our riches cannot make us younger. They can buy us better healthcare, more physical comforts, but they cannot turn back time. Wrinkles and grey hairs do not care how much we despise them. They will come at their time, reminding us to slow down.

### Vanity and the Dread of Losing It
I had crowfeet in my twenties. However, they never bothered me. But now, with other fine lines combined, I see them not as companions of two decades but as invaders in my otherwise smooth and clear skin. My dressing table is full of creams with retinols and peptides, though they make me break out. I hope someday I will not react, and my skin will go back to what it used to be. The search for that perfect hair colour never stops. I change the brand each time, hoping for the one that suits me perfectly and will stay longer.

We are so identified with the vanity that the changes in our appearance can become depressing. Every TV and YouTube commercial that promises to take away a few years from our faces catches our eye. Our talks with friends are now dominated by the skin care treatments. We silently wait for others to praise how our skin still looks taut and mane thick.

### Nostalgia and the Melancholy It brings
As we age, we become wiser, but not necessarily more secure. We have nostalgia for the past, longing to relive some moments. Old pictures bring back the memories of life we once had and the promises we made to ourselves. We realise not all came true — starting the self-conversation of what-ifs. Yet we remind ourselves that the mistakes made us stronger.

### Declining Health and the Need to Slow Down
We find it difficult to accept that we are growing old. Slowly declining health, aches and pains become new friends, making us a little unsettled. We often brush them aside, fearing that it would make us look old. But deep down, we know our body is telling us to slow down, that all will be ok if we listen to it carefully.

### Loneliness and Fear of Death
Old age also sometimes comes with a sense of loneliness. We fear we might be forgotten. Kids grow up and leave. The years that we have spent making our lives all about them come to a standstill. Houses feel emptier, the days are longer, and the future seems uncertain. We feel lost, not knowing where to divert our energy.

Fear of death starts lingering in the background. Though not spoken loudly, it makes its presence felt in fleeting thoughts. Frequent doctor visits, nagging health issues or the death of a close one remind us of our own mortality.

### Ever-Increasing Financial Insecurity
We rejoice in living longer than our forefathers, but this in turn demands more financial resources. After spending on ever-increasing educational costs and weddings of our children, the need to have a substantial amount left for us is another challenge we face today.

We do not want to burden our children, yet the rising costs make us wonder if we are too late to make investments or buy health insurance.

### All is Not Lost
Ageing is inevitable, accepting is crucial. Taking time to imagine our future self can give us clarity on what really matters. Acknowledging that we have lived our youth, and now is the time to welcome the older age, brings peace, contentment and gratitude.

All is not lost. We can still start something new. We can now focus on our passions that we never pursued or even embrace new interests. We can reconnect with the friends with whom we have lost touch or engage more in the community. Simplify life, organise things, clean our mess and choose peace over perfection.

Gracefully, let’s accept who we have become.
