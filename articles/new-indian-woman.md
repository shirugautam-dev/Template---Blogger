# From Good to Real: The Story of the New Indian Woman

## How Choice, Not Obedience, Defines Her

Marinated chicken was waiting for me in the refrigerator. It was already 7 pm, but the office meeting was nowhere near ending. I was checking the time every few minutes, wishing the meeting would end so that I could make dinner.

Finally, it ended at seven-thirty. I looked at my husband, already at the dining table, eating dal chawal from the afternoon.

“Don’t worry. Anyway, I would not eat spicy food at night”, he said.

That was true, but still I felt guilty. I took out the chicken, thinking I should still make it for my son. I was tired from a hectic day. “He too can have dal chawal tonight, tomorrow is the chicken day”, I told myself. I put it back and took out a bowl of khichadi cooked by my husband in the morning and savoured every bite, listening to Shania Twain’s “Honey, I’m Home”, my guilt slowly vanishing into thin air.

In that very moment, I decided to live by choice, not by rule.

When my mother was 19, she was silently fighting with her in-laws to let her wear a salwar-kurta. When I was 19, I was asking my parents to let me move to a different country for higher studies.

Generation after generation, a quiet revolution is unfolding - not fought on streets but at homes and offices.

My mother lived by rules; I live by choices.

And that perhaps is the story of a new Indian woman, not necessarily a rule breaker, but a new rule maker.

### Empowerment and Obedience: Hand in Hand
From childhood, we are told to be obedient. Speak in a certain way, not to laugh too loudly. Do not make unnecessary noises. Moreover, behave like a girl. While in school, I was teased for taking part in a table-tennis match where the other participants were boys. I was always scolded for being loud and opinionated. I had not been rude or disrespectful. Still, I was causing discomfort to the elders around me. I understood early on that only obedience earned praise, not speaking up your mind. We are told stories of Sita and Savitri. Though not direct, the message is clear — be selfless, modest and compliant. Restraint is what we ought to have — of speech, of ambition, of desire.

We are praised for being bold and successful. And yet the invisible conditions tag along. We celebrate the rule-breaker as long as she breaks them politely, and she is not from your home.

Today we say balance, adjustments and values. All these come with the same set of underlying instructions. Our parents want us to be ambitious but remind us not to be aggressive, independent but not intimidating. The society contradicts itself. It wants us to move forward, but not so much that it challenges the authority.

Empowerment is promoted, but with fine print.

### No More Asking, Just Becoming
The new Indian woman is not apologetic, nor is she explaining herself. She is not waiting for anyone’s permission. She is, in fact, informing everyone that she is rewriting the norms.

Be it a single mother building life on her terms, or a CEO who doesn’t flinch in a boardroom full of men, or a founder of a startup who is fighting a cut-throat competition or a student who refuses to abide by the curfew times. We are seeing the changes everywhere.

Also, in small towns and villages, we can see women defying norms. They are fighting to continue their education, learning to drive and dreaming of joining the workforce.

We are getting past the comments of being “too ambitious” for working till late, “too picky” for choosing to remain single, “too selfish” for prioritising the career and “too intimidating” for earning more than the partners. Still, it’s not easy.

This new rule-making is not always dramatic. It can be as simple as refusing to laugh at the sexist jokes or asking her spouse to help her with household chores. It can be empowering another woman at work or walking out of a relationship that demands her to be just nice.

The aim is not to be a rebel, but to live a life of choices. One should not mistake her anger for hysteria or dismiss it as PMS. It’s the power she holds to change the course of her life.

She is choosing what to wear, whom to marry and whether to have children. These choices, when made by millions across the country, the revolution is happening slowly but surely.

### Men: From Saviours to Allies
New women are forcing new men to evolve as well. They, too, are realising that they are the victims of patriarchy as well, punishing them for softness. They, too, are now rejecting toxic masculinity and embracing vulnerability.

They are also valuing consent in sexual relationships and equality in workplaces. Though it is far from being perfect, it is still something. They are sharing domestic load, speaking against harassment and ditching the saviour mentality to become allies, thus helping in rewriting the rules.

### Transformation Alongside Tradition
Empowerment does not mean rejection. Her fight is not against tradition; it is against the limitation. Wearing a saree can be as feminist as rejecting one. Celebrating festivals with all the rituals can be liberating when done with a choice, not as a compulsion. The difference lies in who decides.

That’s what defines the new woman: Choice without justification.

### From Good to Real
The new woman is a rule breaker and a rule maker at the same time. She is breaking old ones to make room for the new ones. She still battles judgements and biases, but she is also armed with an unshakable sense of self.

She wants more — more space, more respect, more choice.

She has been “good”, now she wants to be “real”.
