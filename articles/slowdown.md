# How to Slow Down in a World That’s Running at Full Speed?

Usually, I walk in the evening. We have a reasonable size of green area where I stay. It is well maintained, it is breezy, lighting is good. There is a small pond, and, in the evening, they turn on the fountain. The lights shine in the fountain, making it even more beautiful.

Today I went there in the late morning. There were fewer people compared to in the evening. There was no fountain. The water was calm, and the air was not breezy. Though it was a very bright and sunny day, because of the trees, it did not feel hot. I walked under the shade. While I was walking with different thoughts drifting in and out of my mind, I started hearing birds chirping, saw squirrels crossing the walkway. Usually, I do not pay much attention to these things. But today I decided to open my senses.

Immediately, I felt the difference. I could smell the flowers, hear the birds, and witness the small insects crawling around. Just slowing down for a bit brought the world in front of me differently. The world is not only what I see, but also much more than that. I realized how I have been rushing through my life, not noticing things that were just in front of me all this time.

Technology is making us faster than before. But still, we are running. Shouldn’t it be the other way round? It should have saved us time. Instead, we are running against time. We want everything instant. Instant cooking, instant delivery, instant gratification. We are always available to everything else, checking our messages constantly, responding constantly, but we do not have time for ourselves. We are anxious, always in a hurry and comparing ourselves with others and feeling incomplete.

The need to slow down is apparent. It does not mean we become lazy and do not do anything. Rather, we do things with full intention, going all out.

### What is Hurried Life Doing to Us?
This constant running is making us stretched thin. Our attention is fragmented. We are scrolling social media during our lunch, the person in front of us is doing the same, as if we are not sharing the same time and space. We are checking our emails before going to bed. We are stretching our hands to get hold of the phones that lie just next to the bed as soon as we open our eyes in the morning, as if the world changes while we are sleeping. This “continuous partial attention”, as psychologists say, is making us scattered and keeping us on edge.

We think speed is productivity. It is not. We are compromising quality for quantity. What we require is a free mind to cultivate creativity. We require time and patience to foster relationships. We need rest instead of the constant stress we put our bodies through in this hurried world.

Choosing to be present, being mindful, is how you rebel against this ever-running culture.

### Choosing to Slow Down
Even though the world is running fast, we can still choose to slow down. We can leave our self-imposed busyness and embrace the peace that slowness brings. We can simply stop over-committing.

As Mahatma Gandhi said, “There is more to life than increasing its speed” We need to ask ourselves what we are running after. Most of the time, they are the goals that may not even fulfil us once achieved. Choosing what matters the most and letting go of the rest is the key.

It does not mean that we need not be ambitious or withdraw ourselves from modern life, but we can be more discerning — acting quickly when needed but also learning to pause.

### What Happens When We Slow Down?
In simple words, we become more attentive. We live in present. Our relationships improve. Psychologists argue that there is no such thing as multitasking. We simply cannot do multiple things simultaneously. Multitasking is nothing but doing various things without total engagement.

If we just focus on one thing at a time, like eating without scrolling our phones, listening without the background noise of our mind, working without distraction, we become a better version of ourselves.

### How Can We Really Do It?
Thanks to technology, we get things done efficiently, stay connected and gather information anytime, anywhere. But it has also consumed us. It has hijacked our focus. We are losing track of time.

Slowing down requires us to set a time limit on our net surfing or binge-watching on Netflix or YouTube. We can discipline ourselves to not touch the phones until 30 minutes after we get up and not use them an hour before bed.

Walking in the park, spending some time under the sun, and getting our hands dirty in the garden are natural mind re-setters. They make us more peaceful, reduce stress, improve concentration, and elevate mood.

Resting is the restoration of the mind and body. It is the necessity of a clear mind rather than the reward for hard work. Sleeping well, taking breaks, day daydreaming are healthier than modern life has made us believe.

Let us slow down. Let us not be obsessed with productivity. Let us not measure ourselves by the output we have generated. We are, after all, humans, not machines.
