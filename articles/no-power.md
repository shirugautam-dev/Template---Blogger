# No Gives the Power That Yes Cannot Give

“Do you want to come along?” My husband asked me last Monday. He was invited as a guest lecturer at COEP Technological University, Department of Mechanical Engineering.

I knew my value addition was nil. He might even feel a little self-conscious knowing I was in the audience. More than anything, it was not my subject — I would not understand a thing. I figured I might as well go to the gym and cook a hearty meal by the time he got back.

“No” I answered. I did not give any explanation, and he did not ask for one.

We have become that kind of couple whose nos are more frequent than yeses, but our yeses are more meaningful. We have not reached here overnight. It took us years. These days, our responses to each other are honest, not driven by a need to please.

“NO” is a powerful word.

Learning to say no to things that I did not want to do did not come easily. I still say yes to many things I am not interested in, but the frequency is decreasing day by day.

From childhood, we were all taught to be agreeable. Especially for women, it’s given. Our society still expects women not to argue, say yes and quietly agree to everything.

When we say no, we feel guilty, and we fear disappointing others. Hence, we become “yes” people — yes to everything. And the more we say yes, the harder it becomes to say no.

But the liberation that saying no brings is not small. It gives power back to us. It’s an act of self-respect. It’s not that we are rejecting others, but we are prioritising what is important to us. It is a freedom that we deserve.

### “Yes” Traps Us

“Yes” feels comfortable and easy. We feel helpful and pleasing to others. It gives us a sense of security. Most of the time, we say yes because we are afraid of what will happen if we do not. We fear being disliked, judged or seen as difficult. We want to feel needed and look dependable. What we are really looking for is the approval from others. Slowly, we start living the way people expect us to. We move away from ourselves. We make decisions based on what others think rather than what we want.

But every yes is a trade-off of our time, emotions and mind space. We fill our days with a list of things that we do not really want to do, things that are not even our obligations. We burn out eventually, resentment builds up, and joy disappears.

### The Freedom “No” Brings

Saying no is uncomfortable and difficult at first. But once we say it, we reclaim something priceless — our time. We can decide how best to use it. We get breathing space to think, choose what matters or simply rest. It helps us to protect our mental health.

Once we start focusing on our needs, saying no becomes clearer and necessary even. It is not selfishness. Its integrity. We are choosing to live in alignment with who we really are.

Our worth is not defined by how much we give in to others. We need to stop apologising for wanting to have space. Every time we say no, we are not closing the doors. We are standing for the fact that our needs matter.

Freedom begins when we stop saying yes out of habit and start saying no out of respect for ourselves.

### Respect Earned Through Boundaries

Contrary to what we assume, saying yes when we do not mean it does not earn respect — it often does the opposite. We convince ourselves that a “no” will damage our relationships, but it is dishonesty that weakens them.

Its boundaries, not blind agreement, that earn respect.

When we are sure of our choices, others also begin to trust our words more. They see that we have clear thoughts and intentions. We are not those who can be manipulated or pressured through guilt.

We will have healthier expectations from each other, and our relationship will improve.

### A True No is Needed for a True Yes

We are not a perpetual resource. We all have our limits. When we hide our limits and agree to everything, resentment brews beneath the surface. We give in reluctantly, feel taken for granted, and end up exhausted and unappreciated. When we draw boundaries and clearly state what we cannot do, others start to understand us as well.

Saying no does not push people away. Ironically, it often brings them closer. Our yes becomes more genuine and wholehearted. We stop sacrificing ourselves and stop expecting others to sacrifice for us.

Healthy relationships need honesty, not endless yeses.
